# Finance 101 — A Zero-to-Basics Guide (for a 21-year-old)

Written for someone starting from **absolute zero**. Every concept your app
teaches is explained here in plain language, with real ₹ numbers. Read it slowly,
one section at a time. By the end you'll understand every term in FirstSalary
Quest — CTC, SIP, compounding, EMI, funds, insurance — and, more importantly,
what to actually *do* with your first salary.

> ⚠️ This is **education, not personalized advice**. And you have a superpower most
> people don't: your father has done this professionally for 25 years. Use this
> guide to learn the vocabulary, then take your questions to him — that's the
> fastest way to really get it.

## The one idea behind everything

Money you **spend** is gone. Money you **save and invest early** quietly grows on
its own and can make you free later. Most people get this backwards in their 20s —
they inflate their lifestyle and take loans for cars/phones/homes too early, and
spend the rest of their life paying interest to others. The whole point of
learning this now is to be on the **receiving** side of interest, not the paying
side.

That's it. Everything below is just the details of doing that well.

## 1. Your salary — CTC vs take-home

When you get a job offer, it shows a big number called **CTC (Cost To Company)** —
the *total* the company spends on you per year. But you never receive all of it.
After deductions, what actually reaches your bank is your **take-home (net) pay**.

A rough example of a ₹6,00,000/year (₹50,000/month CTC) salary:

| Term | What it is |
|------|------------|
| **Basic pay** | The core of your salary; many other things are calculated as a % of it |
| **HRA** (House Rent Allowance) | An allowance for rent; can reduce tax if you pay rent |
| **PF** (Provident Fund / EPF) | ~12% of basic, auto-saved for retirement (you + employer both put in). *This is forced saving — a good thing.* |
| **Gratuity** | A lump sum you get after 5+ years at a company |
| **Professional tax** | A small state tax (few hundred ₹) |
| **TDS** (Tax Deducted at Source) | Income tax the employer deducts before paying you |

So **CTC − (PF + gratuity + taxes + deductions) = take-home**. Always budget on
**take-home**, never CTC. *(In the app, this is why your salary input is "monthly
take-home" — the real money you can actually use.)*

## 2. Budgeting — give every rupee a job

A budget is just deciding where your money goes *before* it disappears. A simple
starter rule is **50 / 30 / 20**:
- **50% Needs** — rent, food, transport, bills (things you can't skip)
- **30% Wants** — eating out, subscriptions, fun (nice, optional)
- **20% Savings & investments** — pay this to your *future self first*

The most powerful trick: **pay yourself first.** On salary day, *automatically*
move your savings/investments out — before you can spend it. What's left is what
you live on.

The silent killer is **lifestyle inflation**: as you earn more, you spend more, so
you never actually get ahead. A ₹250 food delivery every night is ₹7,500/month =
₹90,000/year — that alone, invested, is lakhs later.

## 3. Emergency fund — your first safety net

Before investing, before anything fancy: build a pile of cash for emergencies (job
loss, medical bill, urgent repair). Target **3–6 months of your essential
expenses**, kept somewhere **safe and instantly accessible** (a savings account or
liquid fund — *not* stocks, not locked deposits).

Example: if your essentials are ₹25,000/month → aim for **₹75,000 to ₹1,50,000**.

Why first? Because without it, one surprise forces you into a high-interest loan or
to sell investments at the worst time. The emergency fund is what lets everything
else stay on track. **A credit card is NOT an emergency fund** — it's a loan.

🧮 *Formula the app uses:* `target = monthly essentials × months (e.g. 6)`.
🔗 *In the app:* the Emergency Fund calculator + the **"Emergency fund"** lesson.

## 4. Inflation — why you *must* invest

Prices rise over time. What costs ₹100 today might cost ₹107 next year. That means
**cash sitting idle loses value** — the same rupees buy less each year. This is
**inflation**.

So "keeping all my money safe in the bank" is actually a slow, guaranteed loss of
buying power. To at least keep up, your money needs to *earn a return* — ideally
more than inflation. That's the entire reason investing exists: not greed, but to
stop your money from quietly shrinking.

## 5. Compounding — the most important idea in finance ⭐

Compounding = **your returns earn their own returns**. Interest on interest. It
starts slow and then explodes, so the single biggest lever is **time** — starting
early beats starting big.

**See it with numbers.** Invest ₹5,000/month, assume ~12%/year (illustrative, not
guaranteed):

| You invest ₹5,000/month for… | Total you put in | Roughly becomes |
|---|---|---|
| 25 years (age 35 → 60) | ₹15,00,000 | ~₹95 lakh |
| **35 years (age 25 → 60)** | ₹21,00,000 | **~₹3.2 crore** |

Look carefully: putting in just ₹6 lakh more (by *starting 10 years earlier*) turns
~₹95 lakh into ~₹3.2 crore — **more than triple**, for basically the same monthly
effort. That's compounding + time. This is *why* your father says students who
start investing early win — the early years are worth the most.

This is the concept to burn into your brain. Everything else is secondary.

all have great ones).

## 6. The investing vocabulary (funds, SIP, NAV…)

Now the words that scared you — all simple once unpacked:

- **Stock / share** — a tiny ownership piece of one company. Its price goes up and
  down a lot (risky if you own just a few).
- **Bond / debt** — you *lend* money (to a government/company) for fixed interest.
  Safer, lower returns than stocks.
- **Mutual fund** — a basket where thousands of people pool money and a manager
  buys many stocks/bonds. You get instant **diversification** (many eggs, many
  baskets) without picking stocks yourself.
- **Index fund** — a *special* mutual fund that simply copies a market index (like
  the Nifty 50 = India's 50 biggest companies). No star manager trying to beat the
  market; it just tracks it. Usually **low-cost**, and low cost matters a lot.
- **Equity vs debt fund** — equity = mostly stocks (higher risk/return, for
  long-term goals). Debt = mostly bonds (safer, for shorter-term).
- **NAV** (Net Asset Value) — the "price" of one unit of a mutual fund.
- **Expense ratio** — the yearly fee the fund charges (e.g. 0.2% vs 1.5%). Lower is
  better — fees quietly eat your returns over decades.
- **Diversification** — don't put everything in one place; spread risk. A single
  stock can crash; a whole index rarely goes to zero.
- **Risk & time horizon** — money you need *soon* (1–3 yrs) should be safe (debt).
  Money for *far away* (10+ yrs, like retirement) can handle stock ups-and-downs
  and should, to grow.

## 7. SIP — how you actually invest, and its maths

A **SIP (Systematic Investment Plan)** = investing a **fixed amount every month**
into a fund, automatically. That's all it is — a monthly auto-invest habit. It's
powerful because it (a) forces consistency and (b) averages your buying price
(some months you buy cheap, some dear → "rupee-cost averaging").

**The formula your app uses** (future value of a monthly SIP):

```
FV = P × [ ((1 + i)^n − 1) / i ] × (1 + i)

P = monthly amount,  i = monthly return (annual% ÷ 12 ÷ 100),  n = number of months
```

*Worked example:* ₹5,000/month, 12%/yr, 10 years → `P=5000, i=0.01, n=120` →
you invest ₹6,00,000 and it grows to ≈ **₹11.6 lakh**. The extra ₹5.6 lakh is
compounding doing the work.

**Reverse it (SIP for a goal):** "I want ₹5,00,000 in 3 years." The app rearranges
the same formula to tell you the monthly SIP needed (≈ **₹11,500/month** at 12%).
🔗 *In the app:* the **"SIP Growth"** and **"SIP for a Goal"** calculators — this is
exactly `sipCalculator.ts`.

## 8. Debt & EMI — the trap most people fall into

Borrowing brings *future* money into the present — at a cost (**interest**). An
**EMI (Equated Monthly Instalment)** is the fixed monthly amount you repay on a
loan.

**The EMI formula your app uses:**
```
EMI = P × r × (1 + r)^n / ((1 + r)^n − 1)
P = loan amount,  r = monthly interest rate,  n = number of months
```

*Worked example — the "cheap" car:* a ₹10,00,000 car loan at 10% for 7 years →
EMI ≈ **₹16,600/month**, and over 7 years you pay ≈ **₹13.9 lakh** — nearly **₹4
lakh of pure interest** on top of the price. And the car is *losing* value the
whole time. That's why buying a car or big home on EMI *too early* is the classic
wealth-killer your father warns about: it locks your salary into paying others,
right when you should be investing.

**Good debt vs bad debt:**
- Possibly *good*: borrowing for something that grows your income or wealth (e.g.
  education, sometimes a sensibly-priced home).
- Usually *bad*: borrowing for **depreciating wants** — phones, cars, gadgets on
  EMI/BNPL.

**The credit-card rule:** a credit card is *great* if you pay the **full** bill
every month (free short-term credit + rewards). Paying only the **"minimum due"**
is the trap — the rest carries at ~**36%/year**, one of the fastest ways into a
debt spiral. **Never** roll a credit-card balance.

## 9. Insurance — protect *before* you grow

Insurance is **protection against rare but huge financial shocks**. You pay a small
regular **premium**; if a covered disaster happens, the insurer pays the big bill.
Its job is to stop one bad event from wiping out years of savings. The four kinds
in your app:

- **Health insurance** — pays hospital bills. *Get this early*; don't rely only on
  employer cover (it vanishes when you change jobs).
- **Personal accident cover** — pays if an accident affects your ability to earn.
  Cheap; useful if you commute a lot.
- **Critical illness cover** — a lump sum on serious diagnoses (cancer, etc.).
- **Term insurance** — pays your *family* a large sum if you die. Only matters once
  **people depend on your income** (parents, spouse, kids). For a single fresher
  with no dependents, it's usually *not* urgent yet.

**A key learning point (discuss with your father):** independent educators
generally advise keeping **insurance and investment separate** — buy *pure* term
insurance (huge cover, tiny premium) for protection, and invest separately in
funds for growth. Products that mix the two (ULIPs, endowment, money-back plans)
tend to have higher costs and lower returns. Your father can give you the
practitioner's nuanced view on when specific products make sense — this is a
*great* conversation to have with him, and you'll now understand the vocabulary.

## 10. Assets vs liabilities

A simple lens (popularized by *Rich Dad Poor Dad*): over time, does a thing put
money **into** your pocket or take money **out**?
- **Asset** — puts money in: investments, or *skills* that raise your income.
- **Liability** — takes money out: a phone on EMI, a car (fuel + service + EMI +
  it loses value).

Many things people call "investments" are really lifestyle liabilities. A house is
an asset **only if the numbers** (rent saved vs EMI + maintenance) genuinely work.
Spending on **upskilling** early in your career is often one of the best assets,
because a higher income compounds across every future year.

## 11. Taxes — the simple version

Income tax in India uses **slabs**: different portions of your income are taxed at
different rates. So earning a bit more *never* leaves you with less overall — only
the extra slice is taxed higher.

- **Old regime** — higher rates but many deductions (e.g. **80C**: investments like
  PF/ELSS/insurance up to a limit reduce taxable income; HRA for rent).
- **New regime** — lower rates, fewer deductions.
- Which is better **depends on your situation**; people use a simple online
  calculator to compare.

Golden rule: **never spend money just to save tax.** Tax breaks should reward
things you'd do anyway (like investing), not push you into bad purchases.

## 12. Scams & safety (very important for your age)

You are a prime target for money scams. A few rules protect you from almost all of
them:
- **"Guaranteed high returns" = scam.** Real investing never promises assured big
  returns. "Double your money", "assured 5%/month", pressure to "invest now" — run.
- **Tips ≠ advice.** Social-media "finfluencers" are often *paid* to promote things
  and usually aren't registered advisers. Be very wary of anyone selling a specific
  stock/course as a sure thing.
- **Never share OTP / PIN / passwords.** No genuine service asks for your OTP.
- **Beware shady loan apps** demanding access to your contacts/photos.

🔗 *In the app:* the **"Spotting scams"** lesson (and the loan-app decision card).

## 13. The financial "order of operations" (what to actually do)

When you get your first salary, do these **in order** — this is the whole game:

1. **Budget** — know your needs/wants and set a savings %.
2. **Emergency fund** — build 3–6 months of expenses in a safe account.
3. **Health insurance** (+ accident cover) — protect against big shocks.
4. **Clear any high-interest debt** (credit cards, personal loans) — a guaranteed
   "return" equal to the interest you stop paying.
5. **Invest for the long term** — start a SIP into a low-cost index/equity fund,
   automated on salary day. Start small, start *now*, increase it with every raise.
6. **Term insurance** — when someone depends on your income.
7. **Avoid the traps** — don't buy depreciating things (car/phone) on EMI early;
   don't chase hot tips.

Do steps 1–5 and you're already ahead of ~90% of people your age.

## 14. Glossary (quick reference)

- **CTC** — total cost to company; you don't take all of it home.
- **Take-home / net pay** — what reaches your bank after deductions.
- **PF/EPF** — forced retirement saving (~12% of basic).
- **HRA** — house rent allowance (can save tax).
- **TDS** — tax deducted before you're paid.
- **Inflation** — prices rising; cash losing buying power.
- **Compounding** — returns earning returns; grows explosively over time.
- **SIP** — fixed monthly auto-investment.
- **Mutual fund** — pooled, diversified basket of investments.
- **Index fund** — low-cost fund that tracks a market index.
- **NAV** — price of one fund unit.
- **Expense ratio** — the fund's yearly fee.
- **Equity / debt** — stocks (growth, risky) / bonds (safe, lower return).
- **Diversification** — spreading risk across many holdings.
- **EMI** — fixed monthly loan repayment.
- **Premium** — the amount you pay for insurance.
- **Term insurance** — pure life cover for your dependents.
- **Asset / liability** — puts money in / takes money out over time.
- **80C** — a tax section; certain investments reduce taxable income.
- **Emergency fund** — 3–6 months of expenses kept safe and liquid.
